# Reflective Proposal

Through my recent experiments, I found myself most engaged with tracking everyday experiences that usually go unnoticed. In particular, my data drawing of sudden sounds and interruptions made me more aware of how frequently my attention is disrupted. What interested me most was not just the quantity of these events, but how subjective and inconsistent they felt. This aligns with ideas of data humanism discussed in class, where data is not purely objective but shaped by personal perception and experience. My experiments challenged the idea of clean, structured datasets by revealing how messy and interpretive everyday data can be. Moving forward, I want to explore how ordinary moments can be captured and represented in ways that reflect their emotional and experiential qualities, rather than reducing them to purely numerical values.

For my project, I want to focus on the theme of attention and distraction in everyday life. More specifically, I am interested in how our environments constantly interrupt us, often without us fully realising it. I want to explore a future scenario where attention is continuously monitored and quantified, potentially for productivity tracking or behavioural optimisation. In this scenario, everyday distractions such as noise, notifications, or movement could be recorded and analysed to evaluate a person’s efficiency or focus levels. This raises questions about surveillance, datafication, and how personal experiences are translated into measurable data.

The data for this project would be a combination of self-collected and simulated data. I would continue recording interruptions in my daily environment, including their frequency, type, and perceived intensity. This data is subjective and qualitative, which is important to my concept, as it reflects personal experience rather than objective measurement. I may also simulate extended datasets to represent how this system could scale over time or across multiple users. The interpretation of this data would focus on patterns of disruption, but also on how these patterns might be misleading or reductive when removed from context. This connects to course ideas around data representation, particularly how data can simplify complex human experiences and potentially misrepresent reality.

The final visualisation would likely be an interactive digital piece built using p5.js. It could take the form of a dynamic system where interruptions appear and accumulate over time, with the viewer able to interact with or manipulate the data. For example, the audience might be able to toggle between “raw experience” and “optimised data view,” highlighting the difference between lived experience and quantified metrics. The visual style would aim to retain some of the irregularity and imperfection of my hand-drawn work, while also incorporating structure and interaction.

The main provocation of this project is to question how much of our lives can or should be measured. I want the audience to reflect on how data-driven systems might overlook the complexity of human experience, particularly when it comes to attention and distraction. Ideally, the project would encourage viewers to reconsider how they interpret data about themselves and others, and to recognise that not everything meaningful can be easily quantified.
